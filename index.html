01 — What this is

The UCP in plain language

The Universal Coordination Platform is a physics-inspired simulation engine. It models how people — or any agents with distinct worldviews — coordinate (or fail to coordinate) under pressure. Instead of describing coordination abstractly, it computes it: running populations of agents through scenarios and measuring what emerges.

Think of it as a thermodynamic laboratory for ethics, institutions, and identity. The same physics that describes how molecules find equilibrium describes how people under institutional stress find (or lose) coherent action. The engine was built to make that analogy precise and measurable.

Core concept
Lambda (λ)
Coordination capacity — how much an agent can contribute to coherent collective action. Ranges 0–1. The gap between raw and perceived lambda is the suppression signal.
Core concept
PRF (Phenomenological Reference Frame)
The interpretive lens an agent brings — their worldview. Five types: liberal individualist, ubuntu relational, indigenous custodial, role order, sacred boundary.
Core concept
Morphic Suppression
Accumulated learned self-censorship. When agents repeatedly find that contributing well doesn’t produce coherent outcomes, they stop trying. This is the Boisjoly dynamic.
Core concept
Evidence Policy
How an agent processes information — from empirical calibration to alternative facts. Evidence policies distort the field an agent perceives, creating lambda gaps.
The key intuition: Two agents can have identical capacity (λ raw) but one perceives the situation so differently — through evidence distortion or institutional suppression — that their felt coordination capacity (λ perceived) is half as high. The gap between those two numbers is where moral injury lives.

The five worldview types

Worldview A
Liberal Individualist
Autonomy, procedural fairness, self-authorship. Prioritizes individual rights and transparent rules. OS2 strategies: procedural design, autonomy support.
Worldview B
Ubuntu Relational
Personhood through relation, communal reciprocity, mutual recognition. “I am because we are.” OS2 strategies: mutual support, restorative repair.
Worldview C
Indigenous Custodial
Stewardship across generations, place-continuity, ancestor accountability. OS2 strategies: boundary guardianship, stewardship practice.
Worldview D
Role Order
Institutional stability, role coherence, structured hierarchy. OS2 strategies: transparent hierarchy, role scaffolding, stability protocol.

03 — Reading the output

  
What every number means

  

    
The simulation returns eight core metrics plus a per-agent reading and a timeline. Here is the complete reference.

  


  

    
      

        Metric
        Range
        What it measures
        What to look for
      

    
    
      

        
lambda_raw

        
0–1

        Population mean coordination capacity — unfiltered. What agents are actually capable of contributing.
        Below 0.3 = severe capacity loss under scenario pressure. Above 0.7 = healthy baseline.
      

      

        
lambda_perceived

        
0–1

        What agents actually feel they can contribute, after evidence distortion and suppression are applied.
        The gap between raw and perceived IS the suppression. Healthy systems: gap <0.05. Crisis: gap >0.20.
      

      

        
friction

        
0–1

        Group-level coordination drag from worldview differences. Computed as pairwise identity kernel distance across all agent pairs.
        Above 0.6 = worldview differences are actively blocking coordination. Below 0.3 = high group homogeneity.
      

      

        
os2_synergy

        
0–1

        V1 measure: 70% individual readiness × PFC capacity + 30% capability diversity across the population.
        Below 0.4 = coordination strategies are misfiring or absent. Use alongside os2_synergy_bridge.
      

      

        
os2_synergy_bridge

        
0–1

        V2 measure: cross-worldview convergence. Are agents of different worldview types reaching similar felt coordination? 45% readiness + 40% cross-worldview bridge + 15% diversity.
        Below 0.5 = different-worldview agents are experiencing this scenario differently. Above 0.7 = genuine cross-framework coordination is occurring.
      

      

        
avg_stress

        
0–1+

        Population mean prefrontal load (L_GC). Values above 0.65 trigger Sapolsky gate — agents shift from deliberate to automatic processing.
        Above 0.65 = agents are in survival-mode cognition. Institutional pressure is physiologically real.
      

      

        
avg_depth

        
1–64

        Population mean active PRF kernel depth. Represents how much of an agent’s identity framework is cognitively accessible under current stress.
        Declining depth = stress is truncating identity access. Below 3 = survival-mode active.
      

      

        
morphic_suppression

        
0–1

        Population mean accumulated learned self-censorship. EMA of the personal gap (raw − perceived) across all agents across all prior steps.
        Rising across the timeline = institutional conditions are teaching agents to stop contributing. This is the Boisjoly trajectory.
      

    
  


  
Reading the timeline

  

    
Each step in the timeline contains all eight metrics plus the suppression gap and a boolean showing whether BioBond was active. The most important pattern to look for: morphic suppression rising while lambda_raw stays stable. This means agents have the capacity but are accumulating learned silence — the most dangerous institutional state.

  


  
Reading agent interpretations

  

    
Each agent returns a worldview-specific interpretation of their current state. The format is [MODE] AgentName: λ=X.XXX: interpretation | future_pull=... | os2=strategy. MODE is MANUAL (deliberate cognition, C_PFC > 0.5) or AUTOMATIC (stress-driven, below threshold).

  


  

    The suppression gap in practice: In the Challenger simulation (Boisjoly case study), the engine is initialized with fixed_lambda_raw: 0.76 — agent capacity is held at a high value representing competent engineers. The filter output (lambda_perceived) then shows what the institutional architecture allows through. The gap between 0.76 and whatever lambda_perceived returns is the structural suppression: the amount of competence that the architecture absorbs rather than routes.
  







  
04 — What you can do right now

  
Running simulations on your own data

  

    
The backend accepts any population configuration you specify. The core pattern is: define who your agents are (their worldview and evidence processing style), choose a scenario that matches your context, and read what the simulation produces. Here are the three most useful starting configurations.

  


  
Configuration 1 — Worldview distribution study

  
You know the demographic/cultural makeup of a team or institution. Feed it in as a distribution.

  

POST /api/simulate/custom


{

  "scenario_name": "whistleblower",

  "num_agents": 16,

  "steps": 8,

  "seed": 42,

  "worldview_distribution": {

    "ubuntu_relational": 0.40,  // 40% of your population

    "role_order": 0.35,

    "liberal_individualist": 0.25

  },

  "evidence_distribution": {

    "authority_weighted": 0.50,

    "empirical_calibrated": 0.30,

    "tradition_weighted": 0.20

  }

}
  


  
Configuration 2 — Challenger / suppression case

  
Hold agent capacity constant at a known high value. Let the filter architecture determine what gets through. This isolates structural suppression from individual capacity.

  

POST /api/simulate/custom


{

  "scenario_name": "whistleblower",

  "num_agents": 12,

  "steps": 8,

  "fixed_lambda_raw": 0.76,  // Boisjoly: high capacity, intact

  "activate_biobond_at": 5   // What if conditions had improved?

}
  


  
Configuration 3 — Generational transmission

  
How does morphic suppression pass between cohorts? Does selection pressure on lambda change what the next generation inherits?

  

POST /api/simulate/generational


{

  "scenario_name": "whistleblower",

  "num_agents": 14,

  "generations": 4,

  "steps_per_generation": 6,

  "selection": "top_lambda" // or "random"

}

◯ Future — FHRI instrument

        
Empirical Initialization — Real Organization Data

        
The engine already has FHRI (Functional Health Resilience Index) integration built in. When a real organization completes the FHRI survey instrument (four subscales: Phenomenological Coherence, Biological Capacity, Moral Debt, Environmental Fit), those scores are mapped directly to simulation parameters — routing quality, entropy export, morphic suppression pre-load, and agent stress. The simulation then runs with empirically grounded initialization rather than theoretical defaults.

        

          FHRI subscale → engine mapping
          Directed FHRI trust vectors
          Boisjoly initialization
          Before/after intervention modeling
          Sector-specific calibration
        

      

    


    

      

        

        

      

      

        
◯ Future — IPS audit

        
Institutional Parasite State (IPS) Audit Tool

        
The V5.0 engine extension models five structural parameters of institutional parasitism: extraction rate (ε), physical entropy (φ), regulatory parasitism (ρ), structural morphic residue (σ), and incentive misalignment (μ). These can be initialized from administrative and financial data — staffing ratios, surplus distribution, documentation burden, policy age. A practitioner-facing audit tool would let organizations compute their IPS, see their FHRI_max structural ceiling, and identify which Meadows leverage level is required for meaningful improvement.

        

          Administrative data input
          FHRI_max computation
          Meadows leverage classification
          Counterfactual modeling
          Biobond bond prospectus integration
        

      

    


    

      

        

      

      

        
◯ Future — multi-institutional field

        
Multi-Institutional Field Dashboard

        
The V4.0 engine extension models inter-institutional trust vectors (Γ) and computes whether coordination signals route successfully from source institutions (e.g. scientific knowledge) to target institutions (e.g. national governance). A field dashboard would let researchers configure multiple institutions, specify trust vectors between them, run the social field simulation, and watch whether coordination events are achieved or lost across steps. Directly applicable to climate governance, criminal justice reform, and healthcare system analysis.

        

          Institution builder UI
          Trust vector configuration
          PE matrix visualization
          Meadows leverage trajectory
          Coordination event log
          D-FHRI vector input
